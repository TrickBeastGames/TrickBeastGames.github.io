Quantum Nature of Light

Explore the quantum mechanics behind light and the photoelectric effect.

Energy of a Photon (E = hf)

Light is quantized, meaning it consists of discrete packets of energy called photons. The energy of a photon is directly proportional to its frequency:

E = hf

Where:

  • E: Energy of the photon (in joules)
  • h: Planck’s constant (6.63 x 10-34 J·s)
  • f: Frequency of the light (in hertz)
Example 1: Calculate the energy of a photon of green light with a wavelength of 520 nm.
  1. Convert wavelength to frequency: f = c / λ, where c = 3.0 x 108 m/s and λ = 520 x 10-9 m.
  2. Calculate energy: E = hf.
  3. Result: E = 3.81 x 10-19 J.
Example 2: Determine the energy of an ultraviolet photon with a frequency of 8.0 x 1015 Hz.
  1. Use E = hf: E = 6.63 x 10-34 x 8.0 x 1015.
  2. Result: E = 5.30 x 10-18 J.

The Photoelectric Effect

The photoelectric effect occurs when light shines on a metal surface, ejecting electrons. This phenomenon demonstrates the wave-particle duality of light and provides evidence for the quantization of energy.

Einstein’s Equation

The energy of a photon can be used to eject an electron from a metal if it exceeds the work function (W0):

E = h f = W + KE

Where:

  • W: Work function of the metal (minimum energy needed to eject an electron)
  • KE: Kinetic energy of the emitted electron
Example 1: Light with frequency f = 5.0 x 1014 Hz strikes a metal with a work function W = 2.0 x 10-19 J. Calculate the kinetic energy of the ejected electron.
  1. Calculate photon energy: E = hf.
  2. Subtract the work function: KE = E - W.
  3. Result: KE = 1.3 x 10-19 J.
Example 2: If the incident light has a wavelength of 400 nm, and the work function of the metal is 3.1 x 10-19 J, determine if electrons are ejected.
  1. Calculate frequency: f = c / λ.
  2. Find photon energy: E = hf.
  3. Compare E to W: If E > W, electrons are ejected.
  4. Result: E = 4.97 x 10-19 J > W, so electrons are ejected.

Millikan’s Experiment

Millikan verified Einstein’s theory by measuring the stopping potential needed to prevent photoelectrons from reaching the anode in a photoelectric setup. This stopping potential is related to the kinetic energy of the electrons:

KE = Vq

Where:

  • KE: Kinetic energy of the electron
  • V: Stopping potential (in volts)
  • q: Charge of the electron (1.6 x 10-19 C)
Example 1: If the stopping potential is V = 1.5 V, calculate the kinetic energy of the electron.
  1. Use KE = Vq: KE = 1.5 x 1.6 x 10-19.
  2. Result: KE = 2.4 x 10-19 J.
Example 2: Determine the stopping potential required if the kinetic energy of the electron is 4.0 x 10-19 J.
  1. Rearrange: V = KE / q: V = 4.0 x 10-19 / 1.6 x 10-19.
  2. Result: V = 2.5 V.
